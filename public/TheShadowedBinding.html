The Shadowed Binding

Cirilla Fiona Elen Riannon stepped out of the small, dirty hut into the rain and cold. The small peasant family, consisting of two parents, and three children had only paid twenty-five crowns for a nest of endregai that had sprung up on their property. Not unexpected from people who could barely keep fresh food on the table, but still disappointing.

Climbing back onto Kelpie, Ciri winced as the cut on her leg reopened. As she rode away, the horns of an army sounded in the distance. Another fight. Just one more battle in this endless war. Despite the fall of Nilfgaard and the unification of the north under Radovid, some army or another always had time to invade somewhere.

An hour of riding later later Ciri reached the walls of Novigrad. Along the bridge leading into the city stood a row of pyres, blackened with the charred remains of sorcerers, nonhumans, and any men who spoke the wrong way of the Eternal Fire.

Though she lacked most of the usual witcher mutations, her half armor and sword still gave away her profession to anyone who bothered to pay attention. She would never make it in the upper reaches of the city, but down by the docks, no one had any time for their own business, much less anyone else’s. After Geralt and Triss has burned down the guardhouse in the lower city, most of the so-called “witch-hunters” didn’t spend more than the required two circuits in the mud and crowds of dockside.

Pushing through a crowd and entering the Golden Sturgeon, she ducked her head as she walked past two witch hunters drinking at the bar.

“To Hierarch Valern!” one of them cried, raising his mug and drenching himself in the process. “To the pyres of Novigrad!” Both of the hunters burst out laughing and downed their drinks. One of the sailors in the corner stood up and stumbled drunkenly towards the bar.

“Go back to yer rich palacers and fancy rich men drinks, yeh devil-fuckers!” he said, throwing his mug at the hunters. “I didnae come all the from the isles to be oppressed by another fuckin tyrant!”

The two hunters stood up, drawing their swords. The rest of the tavern patrons took a step back, looking away. Nearing the staircase, Ciri paused, her hand momentarily rising towards the hilt of her sword, just barely concealed beneath her hood. One of the hunters looked at her.

“Got anything to say, girlie? Want a piece?” he said, grabbing the drunk sailor and shoving his sword through the sailor’s stomach. Ciri turning away towards the stairs, seething, and helpless. Even if she did stop these two, there would be more. There would always be more, and if any of the other drinkers ratted her out, she might start getting recognized even down here.

“That’s what I thought!” the witch-hunter said, dropping the bloody corpse and turning back to the bar. “Now, innkeeper, how about another of those ciders?”

The Golden Sturgeon had three floors, an upper floor for the refined clientele, the ship owners, merchants, and bankers who had business dockside and wanted a place to drink that was nearby; the main floor for the common riffraff, sailors, drunkards, and travelers off the street; and a lower floor, home to those would rather their business, perhaps their existence, go unnoticed by those who were not involved in that business or existence, or had been told of it by a trusted friend. As Ciri stepped off of the final step, the entire room paused for a second. Most of them had heard the commotion in the room above, and while Ciri may have passed unrecognized by the witch hunters, anyone who frequented the bottom floor knew a witcher when they saw one, and wondered if someone had decided she would be easy prey, a usually lethal mistake.

In years past the lower floor, while not a resplendent palace, had at least looked as though someone went over it with a duster once a month. These days, however, anything that came down merely stayed there, tossed or kicked into whatever corner seemed most convenient. Ciri looked around the room for a second, grimacing, before recognizing the man she was searching for in one of the more brightly lit alcoves along the back wall.

“Nice meeting place you’ve chosen,” she said, sitting across from the man. He wore a simple shirt and trousers, and a wide brimmed hat that cast a deep shade on his face.

The man looked up at her. His nose was scarred almost in two, and where his left eye should have been was a dark glass sphere. “I didn’t ‘av a choice. Anywheres else and they woulda found me be now.” He glanced around the room. “Still migh’, in fact.”

“Who would have found you? Who are you anyways?” Ciri said. “All I know is that Tri-, a friend of mine said that you were looking for the help of a witch, and that you would have a Skelligan Lily on your hat.”

“Aye, those are true,” he said, leaning forward. “But not a witch, but a witcher. And I hear yer the best around.” He sat back. “I aint one of these folk ye see, I’m, how te put this… ehh… I’m a hunter.”

Ciri started. “A witch hunter?” she looked towards the stairs. “And what would a witch hunter. want with a witcher? His next kindling?” She stood up. “I doubt we have much more to speak about. If you’d be so kind as to not alert your friends upstairs, this tavern won’t need to add your corpse to its long list of possessions.”

“Wait!” The hunter stepped out of the seat and dropped to his knees. “The others don’ know I’m ‘ere. None of ‘em want to let this get out, but I, eh, felt guilty I guess. I didn’ join up to murder, just te’ help put things right.”

“Oh, of course,” Ciri said, turning back. “‘Put things right.’ By which you mean killing every nonhuman and magical person, do you? What exactly do you mean by ‘right’?”

“I don’t…”

“Or, wait, was it the part where you took the bodies and burned them in the town square for all to see?”

“I…”

“Or, perhaps, when you dragged my friend Keira Metz up to one of your pyres and spilled her guts before leaving her to burn?”

“I’m sorry ‘bout that, alright? It wasn’t me that done those things. I just ‘eard bout them elves killin’ the merchants on the roads, and thought, well I can do somethin’ bout that. So I joined up, hopin’ to do some good, and now I’m ‘ere!”

“And where is ‘here’ exactly? What did you call me here for? How did you manage to get me here anyway? I was under the impression I was meeting a sorcerer who needed out of Novigrad.”

The hunter flinched. “That’s not exactly a lie, ye were supposed to be doin that. Problem is, our guys got ‘ere first, and now, well, I took my chance with you. See, the higher ups at the church ‘ave been gettin’ into something of late, and, well, I didn’ sign up fer it.”

“Didn’t sign up for what? I thought doing horrible things to people was your job?” Ciri said, biting off the words.

“Are ye lookin’ for me to go somewhere else?” the hunter said, “They’ve gotten into some bad stuff, an’ I just don’ want that on my conscience no more. See, I was headin’ in to talk with the new head guy, and some’ve the top guys, well, they were standin’ around some kinda pit with these black flames comin’ out of it.”

Ciri froze. “Like some kind of magic spell?”

“I guess, but that weren’t the worst of it. See, floatin’ up over the pit was a hole. I didn’ see where it went, and by the Flame I don’ ever want to.”

“So it’s some kind of portal spell. Imagine if it got out that the leaders of the church were practicing magic. The whole city would go up in flames.” Ciri was silent for a moment thinking.

“Why did you call for me? This doesn’t sound like anything dangerous. Just a bit of magic, which I suppose isn’t normal here, but it’s not going to destroy the city or anything.”

The hunter nodded. “Ye’d think that, but I havn’ told the whole story yet. See, when I was backin’ out to not be seen, I saw somthin’ in the corner of the room, like a shadow, but it were real. Like, it were standin’ up and walkin’ about, not followin’ a person like a shadow should. An’, I don’ know, maybe it’s behind this magic thing?”

“So it’s a monster you’re after?”

“I ‘spose. Can ye take care of it?”

Ciri stood up. “Certainly. For the right price. Witchers don’t work for free.”

“Of course, of course. I’ll find ye something. 400 crowns work?”

“I suppose,” Ciri said, turning to leave the tavern. At one point she could have gotten at leave 600 for a job like this. But now that the most dangerous monster anyone saw was a severed drowner head, they were less willing to cough up a livable amount of money.

As she entered Hierarch Square she heard a racket from the tavern behind her. A second later a bolt from a weak crossbow clattered next to her feet. She turned around in shock and saw two more witch hunters standing outside the tavern. One was reloading his crossbow, and the other, back against the tavern wall, was holding a knife to the throat of her informant, who was point at her in desperation.

Ciri ran. A second bolt hit a sign for a small fish shop, and as she entered the square a third flew past her ear. People dodged out of her way. The lights in Novigrad had yet to be lit, and she ran for the darkest alley that she could.

As she dodged into an alleyway, she once again thanked the gods that she had cut her hair short after an encounter with a dragon in another world that had left her with only one eyebrow and a much more ashy hairstyle, real soot and all. If the hunters chased her or asked around, her previous long ashen hair would have stood out from the crowd.

The dark streets of Novigrad offered no place to hide. Witch hunters knew every nook and cranny. If Ciri wanted to hide, she would have to go somewhere that no witch hunter would ever check — Sigi Reuven’s bathhouse. Condemned ten years ago when Reuven, better known as Dijkstra, died, it had developed a reputation for being haunted by his ghost, or perhaps his old troll, whichever the populace decided was the better story at the time. The cult of the Eternal Flame had placed boards across the entrance to prevent entry and sworn never to set foot inside again. Were it not critical to the integrity of the land above it, they surely would have collapsed it by now.

When Ciri entered the alcove beneath the road, she struggled to see the doors in the darkness. When she found them, she took a deep breath and matter-phased through the wood into the bathhouse.

The inside of the bathhouse was damp and musty. The wooden boards that had formed the walls had rotted, leaving behind nothing but mildew and dust. The once-dimly-lit room was now illuminated by nothing more than the glow from Ciri’s phasing, quickly fading away. Safe in the darkness of the old bathhouse, she found a place to sleep and quickly fell unconscious.

Δ

The next morning Ciri crept out of the bathhouse at the break of dawn. The city lights cast haloes through the mist hanging in the air. A few early-risers trudged slowly towards their place of work, and drunkards shambled back from the holes they had been drinking in. Occasionally she would see a pair of witch hunters in the distance, patrolling the lower city. Apparently Hierarch Valern had heard of her presence and ordered a search.

Ciri put her hood up and started walking towards the Oxenfurt Gate. There was no direct way into the church. If she wanted to get to the monster lurking there, she would have to find another way in.

Once she was far enough out of the city to be hidden from the eyes of anyone inside, she summoned her power and warped a book from Kaer Morhen. It was an old leather-bound journal with grease stains and other various markings covering the outside. At the top was dark gray lettering spelling out: Geralt’s Journal. It was the book Geralt had recorded his travels and monster kills in, and he had given it to her the last time she had seen him in Toussaint.

Inside were notes on his last time in Novigrad while he was looking for her, following the tracks of the Wild Hunt. She seemed to recall his telling a story of finding a secret laboratory under the church, and if she could get into it, perhaps it would connect to the upper levels. It was a long shot, but she couldn’t just ride full tilt into the front gate a second time.

Finally, scrawled in the margin of Geralt’s notes on Novigrad, she found a reference to what she was looking for. “Found small cave on the back of temple isle. Guarded by a golem, and used as a cage to tame drowners. Disgusting, but interesting in application. Show to Vesemir?” There was a rough sketch next to it showing a path off the side of the island behind some houses. Ciri sat in the house pouring over the book for a few hours before heading back towards Novigrad.

Δ

The shadow waited in the darkness. It had waited for years for someone to stumble upon it. When, finally, someone had once again opened the hidden entrance, it had prepared. A meal was coming. A juicy, delicious meal. Not like those fish-men in the cold water, or the mouse-birds that drifted around the cave. A full meal, with flesh, and blood, and brains, and when the rock-giant-man had beaten it to a mush, the shadow would feed.

When the meal had entered, however, the shadow was vexed. This was no meal, but a meal-hunter. One with a silver blade. The shadow wept, and faded back into the depths of the cave. By the time it was safe to return, the cave was empty. No more rock-giant-man, no more fish-men, and no more mouse-birds. The stone was barren and cold, and the shadow wept once more. No longer could it satisfy its hunger with the meager offerings of the cave; it would have to travel upwards through the crevices to find a meal among the fire-men of the brick-cave above. And find a meal it did. Not just the flesh of a man, but its mind. A young, aspiring priest named Eveliina Valern.

The shadow took her mind and made it its own. Her thoughts, its thoughts; her actions, its actions. And when the hierarch of the city of Novigrad died, the shadow placed Eveliina ready to succeed him.

Now the shadow waited once more in its cave. Eveliina had the place-door to its home almost open. It only had to wait a little bit longer. And just as it thought it felt the connection, another meal stepped into the entrance of the cave. And not just any meal, but another meal-maker with a silver blade, and the same silvery hair.

Δ

Ciri crept into the cave, sword in hand. Geralt had claimed to have killed the golem and drowners that guarded the cave, but it had been fifteen years since he had been here, and there was every chance that something else had moved back in.

The dimly lit walls of the cave towered above her as she slowly and carefully moved through the cave. Towards the back she could just barely see a doorway which, according to Geralt, lead into the laboratory of the mad sorcerer Ireneus var Steingard. Here he had experimented on drowners asses their intelligence, and here he had broken and bound a witcher named Kiyan.

If the sorcerer’s notes were to be believed, he had successfully transferred a dark spirit into the former witcher, turning him into an unstoppable force of destruction. When Ciri had read this she had laughed. If he indeed succeeded, then Geralt would almost certainly not have been able to kill Kiyan as he had.

Reaching the door, Ciri cast one last look back towards the mouth of the cave before nudging the door open and stepping inside. The laboratory was lit by magelights in glass spheres, still burning despite their age. The bricks that formed the walls and ceiling were mossy and cracked from the damp sea air.

Somewhere in this lab Ciri hoped there would be some method of access from the church above. It was built in a similar style to the old parts of the church, and based on some elven ruins that would certainly have been studied by the early church, before their descent into fanaticism. If there was a connection, it would be well hidden. The sorcerer had indicated no love for the goings on above, and would have preferred isolation.

As she was searching the ruins of the laboratory, stepping carefully over shattered glass and spilled ingredients, the lights in the room began to flicker. Ciri stopped by a magelight on a tabletop, and watched as it fluttered for a moment before fading into darkness. At the same time, she felt an unmistakeable presence behind her.

A shadow rose out of the darkness. Vaguely resembling some horrid vampire, it lunged across the room, nearly catching Ciri as she spun to the side, sliding her sword out and into a guard position.

The monster growled, and bent its legs to lunge again. As it did so, she stepped through space behind it and slashed at its legs. Quick as a cat it froze mid leap and spun at her, slashing through the bookshelves over her head as she ducked and rolled to the side.

The two of them stood staring at each other for a second. Ciri was baffled. This was not some known creature, not a Hym or an Elder Vampire, this was something new. Something unknown was living under Temple Isle, and it was something she did not know how to kill.

The creature grew tired of watching and sprung at Ciri once more. She dodged around it, and once it was no longer between her and the exit, turned and ran. It chased her, but stopped at the laboratory door, pawing at the ground like a dog.

Δ

Ciri sat down next to a small campfire built inside of an abandoned alchemist’s hut outside of Novigrad. Summoning Geralt’s journal for a second time, she started looking through the section that contained the unknowns. He had run across several creatures in his travels that had confounded him, and had done his best to note them down.

Most of theme were clearly unrelated, mage-created beasts and various sea creatures from Skellige, but two of them caught her eye. Geralt had run across something in the mountains of Nilfgaard that appeared to be an elder vampire, but upon approaching it, it turned into a liquid and tried to suffocate him. It had, however, eventually reverted to a more human form and tried to kill him the standard way, with its claws and fangs. Its physical description sounded similar, but the thing Ciri had met had made no attempt to morph into a liquid.

The other one was something Geralt had met under the halls of Aretuza, long after its collapse. It had possessed one of the last mages trying to reside there and was twisting them into its servant. Geralt had been forced to kill the mage to even get to it, and when he finally tracked it down in the tunnels, it fled into the night, leaving him none the wiser as to what it truly was.

Ciri stared into the fire puzzled. She wasn’t aware of anyone in the city who might be controlled by something like that, especially after Hierarch Valern had outlawed all use of magic. If the creature wanted a mage, it would be better off finding one elsewhere.

Ciri, however, didn’t have that luxury. If she wanted to find someone with magical abilities in Novigrad, there was only one place she could go: Temple Isle.

Δ

The next day was gray and cold. Rain fell intermittently from the sky, drenching the city and sending everyone scurrying indoors. Ciri had stopped by the house of one of Geralt’s old friends, Elihal, just outside the walls in order to get some clothes that would help her blend in at the church of the Eternal Flame. Instead of her usual shirt, leggings and gauntlets she was wearing rough leather pants and a wool sweater.

Her sword was stashed in the box at Kaer Morhen where she could summon it to her in a second, and the knives she usually carried were hidden in a secret sheath in her boots.

The guards at the entrance to the temple waved her in without even looking at her. Inside the temple was quiet. The “original eternal flame” burned upon the altar. The church claimed it had never gone out, but most people knew that this was the fourth or fifth “eternal flame” that had burned in the church.

Looking around, Ciri saw one of the church leaders pacing along the left-hand wall, muttering. Shuddering at the act she was about to put on, she slouched a little bit, tried to look less sure of herself, and approach him.

“Excuse me, archbishop?” she said.

He stopped pacing and looked at her with a disapproving frown. “Yes? What is it girl? I’m quite busy here.”

“I’m concerned,” She said, trying to act timid. “I’m just worried about any magic users that might make it into Novigrad. Are you sure we’re safe here?”

“Of course we are. If a mage made it in, someone would report it, and they would be burned on the pyres. You don’t need to be concerned girl. As long as Hierarch Valern leads, we’ll be safe.” he said. Ciri nodded and began to walk away. As she was walking, she heard him mutter quietly, “If only she would leave that damn room of hers. What’s she need a lead room for anyway?”

Ciri sat down for a moment in the middle of the church. She had been that person once, scared and cowering, and it was unpleasant to return, even faking it. Sitting in the Church of the Eternal Flame hit almost too close to home, and Ciri found herself running out of the building into the courtyard. It was hardly better, given the past events there, but at least it had open, albeit wet and rainy, air.

Unsettled by her experience in the temple, Ciri returned to the alchemist’s hut and re-lit the fire. Her original goal of investigating the temple seemed insignificant after her encounter in the cave. As she thought that she realized: she had been hired to figure out what was happening with a portal spell cast by the leaders of the church. Perhaps Valern herself had been involved. And if they were doing magic, it might have attracted this shadow. Possibly it was even the same shadow the temple guard who hired her had seen.

Ciri debated trying to see if he was still alive to ask him if it was the Hierarch he had seen casting the spell, but she doubted it. He had betrayed them, and failed to given them enough warning to catch her when confronted. He had likely already been burned, and if not, she wasn’t going to have any success breaking in to talk to him.

A lead room. There was some significance to that, Ciri knew. She had seen one once, in the dungeons of Beauclair, but had never asked what its purpose was. Anna Henrietta had breezed by it, only noting the expense required to construct it and saying nothing of its purpose.

Ciri took out Geralt’s notebook for the third time. His notes on scientific matters were minimal, but there was a chance he had seen the very room in question. However, after twenty minutes of searching through references to laboratories full of cheese and the brewing exploits of one of Geralt’s friends (which included mandrake root brews, elvesbane teas, and an attempt to make a variant of Toussaint Est Est with yellow grapes from Kaedwen), she had found no reference to a room made of lead.

Ciri was alone. No information from Geralt, and no way to contact any of the mage she knew for help. The shadow beast she had encountered in the laboratory was presumably still there, preventing her from returning, and the heads of the Eternal Flame were practicing magic. This had gone from a simple contract to find a kill a monster to a problem plaguing all of Novigrad.

Ciri debated walking away. After the death of the guard who hired her she was no longer getting paid, and it would be easy for her to leave and look for work elsewhere. Finding it would be another matter. It had been some time since a witcher had visited Skellige, so she might find work there, though Queen Cerys had expressed no desire to see Geralt or any of his acquaintances ever again after their fight with the Wild Hunt on Undvik.

But it wasn’t that easy. The people of Novigrad had been tormented by the Eternal Flame for years, and now the Hierarch herself was practicing magic. When Geralt had encountered this monster, he had gone to some trouble to try and track it down, but had unfortunately failed. If he considered it dangerous enough to spend months trying to find it, it was clearly worth the time to kill.

Δ

Ciri strode quickly into the courtyard of the church. She had ditched the clothes from her disguise and was counting on the rain and thunder to keep people away from the church long enough for her to reach the Hierarch.

She snuck though the church looking for the lead room the archbishop at mentioned. Every few minutes she had to duck behind a brazier to avoid the guard patrols, but apart from them the building was empty. Ciri had searched the entire building for an hour before she turned a corner straight into some guards. Both she and the guards froze for a moment before they drew their swords and advanced.

Ciri turned and ran, back through the twisting passages in the building. She found herself in the main hall, up on the stage behind the altar. As she looked around in panic for a place to hide, she saw a trapdoor just behind the altar, hidden from the rest of the room.

She had barely closed it behind her before she could hear the guards clatter onto the stage above. They paused for a moment, then turned around and headed back into the passages, assuming she must have jumped down into the audience hall.

The ladder under the trapdoor led down at least fifty feet. Ciri could feel the air turning damp and cool. At the bottom she looked around and saw that she was in a passage similar to those of the laboratory under the island, though this one looked as though people had been using it recently. The magelights that had been in the wall alcoves had been smashed on the floor and replaced with clusters of candles and the occasional lantern.

At the end of the hall was a door, covered in a silvery metal. Ciri walked up to it, drew one of her knives, and scratched it across the door, leaving a long mark. It was lead, soft enough to be scratched by the bone-forged steel of her knife.

Inside she could hear someone chanting. It sounded like Aen elvish, the language used in most magic conjurings. The door was unlocked, so Ciri drew her sword, opened the door and stepped inside.

It was a square room, completely lined with lead, and lit only by a single large brazier in the middle of the room, glowing with a blue flame. Behind it stood the Hierarch of Novigrad, Eveliina Valern, a tall, blonde woman with short hair wearing the robes of a Hierarch, holding a torch. She was tensed, learning against the brazier with her spare arm.

The door swung shut behind Ciri as she entered the room. Hierarch Valern relaxed as the door swung shut. She placed the torch in one of the holders on the side and stepped out from behind the fire.

“Well, this is quite a pickle, isn’t it?” she said, gesturing at the room around them. “We’re both trapped. If I try and leave, you’ll kill me, and if you leave, the shadow will get you.”

Ciri began circling the fire. “What shadow?”

Eveliina laughed. “Oh surely you’ve met it? That black thing that always crawls around the edge of my vision? I know you’ve been down here before. Not into this room, but in the main laboratory. I saw the remnants of your fight there. It made sure I did.”

“What do you know about?” Ciri said. “Did you conjure it?”

“Me? Conjure it? Of course not. Do you take me for a fool? I don’t know what it is, but I know it’s not supposed to be here. I’ve been trying to be rid of it for months now. The other church members don’t understand. They think I’ve gone mad, retreating into this room down here. But it’s the only place the creature can’t come, at least with the door closed.”

“I thought silver stopped monsters, not lead.”

“I wouldn’t know, “ Eveliina said. “I just lucked into finding this room, already built like this, and found out that while I was in here with the door closed, that thing couldn’t do anything to me.”

Ciri looked around the room. The walls, which had a first appeared to be blank and smooth, were actually covered with small carvings: glyphs, drawings, and some blocks of writing almost as if…

“It’s no use trying to read them,” Eveliina said. “I’ve tried. I had the best scholars in Novigrad look at them, copies of course, and they found nothing. I even sent a small piece to the Lodge anonymously, hoping they could decipher it.”

Ciri looked closer at the writing. It looked suspiciously similar to the spells used in the mad sorcerer’s attempt to bind a dark spirit to the old witcher in the laboratory down below.  She turned back to the Hierarch. “If this room can keep out the shadow, I think it may have been used during the summoning process. You know about the sorcerer’s experiments, yes? The one with the witcher in particular.”

Eveliina nodded. “I’ve skimmed his notes, yes. I was under the impression he failed, given that the witcher’s bones are in that chamber.”

“I’m not so sure,” Ciri said. “The witcher actually survived whatever experiment was done, and was only killed about five years ago. And that shadow monster—what if that was the spirit that was summoned? What if it wants a new host?” She gestured at the room. “The lead must keep it out somehow. Maybe it can kill it too.” She turned towards the door. “I recommend you stay here.”

Eveliina grabbed her arm before she could open the door. “Wait! If you open that it will get both of us. It’s not here for you, it just wants you out of the way. It wants me. Wants me do something. If you open that door, it gets it.”

Ciri paused. “Wants you to do what?”

“I’m not sure. I just, loose time on occasion. I’ll sit down by the flame, and suddenly be down here, or walk into a room and immediately end up on the far side of the church. Once I even found myself  in a room with a mage. If I hadn’t been so confused I would have strung him up, but he ran before I came to my senses.”

“Regardless, if this thing dislikes lead, I think I can find a lead dagger somewhere in the city. You have made it somewhat easy to find tools used in killing magic people.”

“How are you going to get out? It doesn’t like me coming in here. I tend to lose time as soon as I leave, so, it’s probably waiting just outside. You won’t get past it.” She slumped against the wall.

“Like this.” Ciri grabbed Eveliina’s arm, reached for her power, and stepped into the abyss. A blinding flash of light lit the room and the two of them vanished.

They appeared inside the burned ruins of the lower guardhouse, startling an owl which had made its home in what remained of the rafters. Ciri quickly looked around to make sure they hadn’t been seen, but Eveliina groaned, took one step, and fell to the ground.

Ciri turned around. “Get up! We don’t have time for this. I have to find a dagger, and you have to lure that creature out of the city so I can kill it without being seen by your guards. Up!”

“What the hell?” Eveliina said, trying to sit up. “Feels like I was turned inside out and thrown into the ocean.”

“It’s normal,“ Ciri said, putting her hood on and stepping out of the building. “You’ll get over it soon enough. Find your way to the greenbark grove. I’ll meet you there.” She turned and stepped into the streets of Novigrad, leaving Eveliina sitting against a wall, hidden in the shadows of the ruined building.

On the south side of town, at the edge of a square with one of the Eternal Flames in the center, Ciri found a small dumpling shop set back into the side of a larger building. She walked up to it and knocked on the door. A minute later it creaked open and a young girl peeked out. “Did you need something, miss?” she said, keeping the door open only an inch.

Ciri glanced around for guardsmen, then leaned in closer and said, “I’m looking for Hattori. Is he still here?”

The girl looked behind her for a second, then turned back, nodded silently, and unlatched the door, opening it just wide enough for Ciri to enter. The inside of the shop was starkly different from the exterior. Where one might have expected a kitchen, with pots and grilling surfaces, it instead was an array of weapons and forging materials, with only a small cooking area along the front wall, facing the outdoor seating area. The young girl, after closing the door behind Ciri and triple checking the lock, ran into a back room and shut the door behind her. In the back corner of the room a man with gray hair sat on a short stool, watching her with piercing blue eyes.

“Greetings, Éibhear,” Ciri said, moving to sit on another nearby stool.

“Zireael. What have you come for? I thought this city was no longer accepting to your profession?”

“I could say the same for you. What if the temple guard found you here?”

“Swordsmiths? They have nothing against swordsmiths. In fact, they make use of my services quite often, though Jess,” he gestured towards the room the young girl had disappeared into, “usually does the actual exchange.”

“You know what I mean Éibhear,” Ciri said. “But that’s not why I’m here. I need a dagger made of lead. How quickly can you make one?”

The elf shifted in his seat, then slowly stood up and limped over to the back wall. “I have one here somewhere…” he said, running his hand over different weapons hanging on the wall. “Ah, here it is!” He took a small dagger off the wall and presented it to her. “Remember not to touch the blade, Zireael. And to clean your hands if you do.”

Ciri rolled her eyes. Elves, especially the Aen Seidhe, had some strange ideas about certain materials.

Hattori turned to her suddenly. “Don’t be so dismissive of our knowledge, eh? We see more of the world than your kind do, does it surprise you that we would know more of its workings? Avoid the blade.” He nudged her towards the door. “You should be going. I think this is enough interaction with humans, even one as pleasant as you, for the time being.”

“How much do I owe you?” Ciri said, pausing to take out some money.

“Just take the dagger. Geralt helped me out of enough troubling situations for anything you need to be worth my time. Now go, please. Ciri opened the door and stepped outside. As she did, Jess emerged from her room and came to lock the door behind her.

Ciri placed the dagger in its sheath and attached it to her belt, then stepped out into the square.The rain, which had paused while she was in the church, was returning and she headed for the city gates quickly, hoping to make it to the clearing before it started pouring.

Greenbark grove was a small stand of trees along the southern edge of Novigrad. The Scoia'tael had used it as a base of operations before Eveliina had called King Radovid to have them cleared out, and the forest burned. Now it was an empty clearing in the woods with gray, scorched trunks of dead trees standing like gravestones.

The clearing was empty. Eveliina was nowhere to be seen. Ciri entered the clearing cautiously, alert for anything out of the ordinary. The dirt crunched under her feet as she moved slowly around the edge of the stand of burnt trees.

As she circled to the back of the clearing she saw words carved into the trunk of one of the burnt-out trees. It was a note from Eveliina, describing how she couldn’t leave the city while the shadow was possessing her. She was instead waiting in the laboratory in the caves, the same place Ciri had first encountered the shadow.

Cursing her luck, and Geralt’s bad note-taking, she set off for the city. The shadow must be unable to leave the place where it was bound, and it must have been bound, at least somewhat, to something under Temple Isle. Perhaps the sorcerer had been more successful than she had thought. Kiyan’s skeleton was still in the chamber, and if the binding was even halfway done, it could have trapped the shadow in Novigrad.

She reached the bridge the crossed to Temple Isle and pushed past the guards standing at the end, only to find the entire bridge blocked by a swarm of temple guards, standing around a group of bodies lying in the road.

She tried to step to the side and sneak past them, but her action of pushing through the guards had already caught their attention. They quickly surrounded her, raising their weapons. The guard commander, a young man with blonde hair, a light beard, and wearing a leather uniform instead of regular armor, stepped out in front.

“What do you think you’re doing here?” he said, holding his hand out to stop her. “Some kind of elf, are you?”

Ciri stopped, and reached for her power. She almost had it, the void welcoming her, when one of the guards behind her closed a dimeritium shackle around her left wrist, cutting off her powers. She fell back into reality, surrounded by guards, all with weapons pointed at her.

“Trying to conjure, eh?” the commander said. “Get ‘er in chains!” The guard behind Ciri tried to get her other wrist in the shackles, but she spun, kicked him in the groin, and dove between him and the guard next to him, getting herself enough space to draw her sword, spin around another guard, and cut him across the back.

His armor deflected the blow, but at that point she was safely out of the circle, facing the entire group of guards on the bridge. However, this time she was on the island side of the bridge, so she did the only logical thing — she turned and ran. The temple guards, momentarily shocked by her flight, were spurred into action by their commander shouting, “After her, whoresons!”

For the second time in a week, Ciri found herself chased though Novigrad by the Eternal Flame. This time they didn’t seem to have any crossbows, but there were a lot more of them. She dove left into an alley and found herself at the edge of a cliff. The path to the cave was farther back along the road, and the guard was too close behind her.

Pausing to make sure none of the guards saw her, Ciri jumped. She hit the ground ten meters below and rolled, straight into a rock. Shit. She groaned and sat up, rubbing her back, and looked around. Something was different.

The cave entrance, formerly a large opening in the rocks, was gone. It had been covered by an illusion, and, as Ciri tried to walk though it, only to trip over the rocks at the base, clearly a solid illusion. She started to draw her power to break it down, before realizing that the dimeritium shackle was still on her wrist. She could cut the chain and other shackle off with her sword, but without the key she had to way of taking it off without hurting herself. Ciri was going to have to fight the shadow without her abilities.

Ten minutes later the guards had moved on to search the lower city, and Ciri snuck up into the church, finding the trapdoor she had entered before and climbing back down into the catacombs.

The tunnels were dark. The candles had gone out, and only one or two surviving magelights were casting a dim glow from distant corners. Ciri, sword drawn, crept carefully through the dark hallways, hoping to find a connection to the laboratory below. She passed the lead room, where the door had been torn off and thrown into the room. Claw marks ran along the hallway farther into the tunnels. She followed them, nearly losing sight of them, before they turned into a broken part of the wall, revealing the cave just on the other side of the illusion at the entrance.

The shadow was angry. Years of waiting, years of planning, now about to be destroyed by another of the white-hairs with the silver swords. It had taken its prey into the cave, sealed the door, and now it waited. It had learned much from the woman it possessed. It knew now how to kill a white-hair. So it waited, and it watched. The white-hair would come. And then it would die. And then, finally, the shadow could go home.

Ciri stepped carefully into the cave. Nothing moved. The door to the laboratory, previously half closed, had been torn off its hinges and thrown to the side, similar to the lead room above.

She approached the door. The magelights inside were dim and flickering. Bones covered the floor. She stepped inside. Nothing moved. Kiyan’s skeleton lay still on the floor. The bookshelves loomed over the room. Shadows from broken pieces of furniture flitted across the walls.

Something moved. Behind Ciri, Eveliina Valern, Hierarch of Novigrad, stepped out of a shadow along the wall and pushed a stone column in front of the door, blocking the only exit from the room. She spoke, but the voice wasn’t hers. It was deeper, and echoed throughout the room, disrupting the dim lights as it moved.

“You will not take her from me, white hair,” it said. “I have seen one of your kind before. He almost ruined my plans. It will not be so with you.” The shadow emerged from Eveliina’s body, dropping her to the floor. It growled, Ciri raised her sword, and the shadow attacked.

Ciri spun out of the way cutting at the shadow, but, like before, it was too fast. It stopped, slashed back at her, and dove, throwing her to the ground, and sending her sword clattering across the room. It raised its claw, growled in victory, and slashed down at her. Using all of her strength, she shoved herself to the side, and the shadow’s claws dug into the stone by her side. Ciri held out her hand, expecting her sword to jump into it, before realizing that her powers were still blocked by the shackle.

The shadow dove again, but this time Ciri spun around it, and snatched her sword off the ground where it had fallen. The two of them stood facing each other. We’ve been here before…, Ciri thought. Something needs to change.

The shadow dove again, but this time she didn’t try and step out of the way, but instead raised her blade and ran it straight through the shadow’s head. It was undeterred. At the last second, Ciri dove out of the way, but its claws caught her wrist, slashing up her arm and spilling blood across the ground.

Ciri hit the ground, screaming in pain. It took a second for her to realize that the shadow, no longer trying to attack her, had been thrown across the room and was pinned to the wall. It’s vicious attack, ripping open her arm, had also cut straight through the dimeritium shackle, dropping it to the ground. Her powers were back.

The force keeping the shadow back had stopped, but it stayed on the far side of the room, creeping slowly away. Ciri picked up her sword for the second time, and forcing the pain out of her mind, paced towards the shadow.

The shadow backed away, and, seeing Eveliina collapsed on the floor, re-possessed her, dragger her to her feet, and once again speaking in its rhythmless voice, “I cannot be killed white-hair. Would you slay your friend in your vain attempt? I have been freed that way before.” It stopped, considered for a moment, then lunged at Ciri with Eveliina’s body, goading Ciri into attacking.

Once more, she spun to the side, and, as she used her hand to push the shadow-Eveliina fusion aside, felt what it was to be the shadow. Fear. Longing for a home it had not known for centuries. The pain of having been summoned, years ago, and trapped, freed, and trapped again. And the overwhelming hatred for those who continued its imprisonment here.

Both of them staggered back. Ciri was shocked. She had never considered that the shadow might want anything beyond power, or further possession of her world. But if all it wanted was to go home, she could give it that. She knew it—a world of mists and trees. She had seen in before, while fleeing from the Wild Hunt.

Reaching for her power, she took the fusion’s hand and stepped into the abyss.

Cirilla Fiona Elen Riannon, lady of space and time, slipped out of the city of Novigrad at midnight. Her job was done. The shadow haunting the city was gone, not killed, simply returned to its home. The Hierarch, as despicable as she may have been, was safe, returned from the shadow’s world with no further signs of the possession. She had not offered Ciri anything in exchange, and had barely even considered telling the guards to let Ciri leave. So Ciri had snuck out.

Her horse, Kelpie, was waiting for her at the alchemists shack. Wincing as she forgot to use her uninjured hand to climb up, Ciri set off towards the south, heading for the Duchy of Toussaint. She had quite a story to tell this time.
